Student: I'm confused about the difference between bias and variance in machine learning. Can you help explain it?
AI Tutor: Great question! Let me explain with a simple analogy:

Bias is like using a ruler to measure something that's actually curved - your tool is too simple for the problem.

Variance is like having a very sensitive scale that gives different readings for the same object - your model changes too much with small data changes.

The goal is to find the right balance - not too simple (high bias) and not too complex (high variance). This is called the bias-variance tradeoff.
Student: That makes sense! Can you show me an example with actual numbers?
AI Tutor: Absolutely! Here's a concrete example:

High Bias, Low Variance: Linear regression on complex data
- Bias: 15% (model too simple)
- Variance: 3% (consistent predictions)

Low Bias, High Variance: Complex neural network with small dataset
- Bias: 2% (model fits well)
- Variance: 20% (unstable predictions)

The sweet spot is usually somewhere in between!
